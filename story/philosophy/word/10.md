## §0 Just words

Everything is a word (word.word), and every word has its **host**: the code or bytes behind it that make it work (9.md §2, the bottom turtle). being is a word, being.js is its host. A matter is a word, its bytes are its host. There is no word without a host.

That kills two terms we tried on the way here:

- **symbol** (a word plus content) is drift. Every word already has content behind it, so "symbol" describes every word and names nothing. A word and its host are just a word.
- **lexicon** (a set of words) is drift. It is a dressed up name for words. We say words.

The only distinction that survives is the one 9.md already drew: the word (declared) and its host (implemented). Not two kinds of thing, two faces of every word. The layout below makes those two faces two folders, and §2 is the clean binding between them.

## §1 The layout: the seed is the factory

The seed IS the factory, the whole apparatus that makes and runs the world. There is no `seed/factory/` subdir, that would be the factory inside the factory. Its ingredients sit directly under `seed/`:

- **words/** = `seed/words/`. The language ingredient: the .word declarations, all of them, flat, word.word at the root. This is the innerStore, your store of words. The other lane is filling it now (being.word, branch.word, name.word, and the rest).
- **materials/** = the kinds (being, space, matter, name, branch, ...).
- **past/** = the record and the tense (facts, acts, chains).
- **present/** = the processing (the stamper, the beats, the word machinery).
- the **word wrappers** live with the machinery they wrap: the connecters that bind each word to its matter (§2).

All of that is the factory; words/ is one ingredient of it, not a thing apart from it. The word and host split is real, but it runs BETWEEN ingredients of the one factory: the words declare, the machinery implements, the connecter binds. They are not merged into one folder, because the wrapper binds them where they are (§2), so a .word and its .js need not share a directory.

- **share/** = `share/` (was `resources/`). Outside the factory: the exchange, empty by default on a fresh land. Words cross it both ways, import IN after boot, share OUT after building. `share/catalog/` (was `resources/store/`) is the browse and install marketplace over it.

So: the seed is the factory, words/ within it is the innerStore (your store of words), and share/ outside is the exchange. A land is born with the whole factory; the share starts empty.

Now versus the full OS. In the JS version the share is a real `share/` directory whose words auto import into the innerStore at boot (the loader does this today). Ultimately there is no separate place: the share is just a LABEL on words already in your innerStore, marked by a fact the way peering is on or off by a fact (§3). The share view becomes a fold of the words wearing that label, not a folder.

## §2 The connecter: binding a word to its matter

A word declares; its matter does the work; a wrapper binds them so the declared word runs off the code. That wrapper is the connecter, and it is the load bearing primitive, because three groups use it: a few core words now, a lot of store packs (a pack is words plus the matter they bring), and people building in world, live.

It is all matter. The host behind a word is matter, code is a matter of type code, a resource pack is matter. So "connect code to a word" is not a new operation, it is "attach matter to a word," and matter is already first class: CAS, types, the content store, per type ops. We are not inventing the connecter, we are making the word to matter binding explicit and uniform, so the same move works for a seed word, a store pack, and a person in world.

This is why the words and the machinery live apart. The binding is the wrapper, not file adjacency; the .word does not need to sit next to its .js. It is also why dropping "symbol" was right: a symbol was "a word plus content," exactly "a word bound to its matter," and since every word is that, it is not a special kind, it is the universal connecter under every word.

Three rungs, one mechanism:

- **boot**: wordFold binds the core words at boot. It already folds word.word and verbs.word via FOUNDATION_WORDS and declarePast; extend it to register all the .words at once as declare word facts (9.md §5/§6). This is the single piece of real wiring, not a connecter per word.
- **import**: a store pack binds its matter when it lands in the innerStore from the share.
- **in world**: a person binds matter to a word with a **do**. That is "executability is a fold" made first class, and do.word is where the verb lives.

### The shape a word expects from the host

A word is live when it answers the verbs, so the host hands up one binding descriptor:

```
bindWord(name, {
  ownerExtension: "seed",                  // identity and provenance
  do:   async (ctx) => result,             // ctx = {target, params, identity, moment, branch}
  see:  (ctx) => faceContribution,         // render hints, content kinds, advertised ops
  be:   async (ctx) => result,             // closed lifecycle slot
  can:  [{verb:"do", word:"set-mail"}],    // a composite word grants, it does not handle
  args: { field: {type, label, required} },// declarative schema (drives forms and auth)
  authAction, matterTypes, targets,        // the gates
  reducer: {initial, reduce, isGone},      // only if this word is a KIND
})
```

Three parts: identity (name and owner), the verb answers (a handler per verb, or a `can[]` grant set for a composite word, or a reducer for a kind), and the gates. A new op fills the do slot; a new matter type fills the see face plus its ops; a new word with code is a handler under its verb; a composite word is just the `can[]`.

Everything here is matter, so a handler is not really an inline closure, it is a **code matter** (a matter of type code, in CAS); the inline `do: async` form is the seed special case where the matter is bundled in the build. A model is a model matter the same way, and `do` binds the word to whatever matter answers it, seed bundled now, CAS resolved in world. And because a word can be nothing but a `can[]`, words STACK: a `user` word (`{ can: [do set-mail, see profile] }`) carries almost nothing itself, it is assembled from other words; an `admin` word is a stack of can delete-user, can create-user, can see-audit-log. Code becomes a word (`send-email`), a model becomes a word (a gpt matter), and a composite word bundles them. That is the whole point: stack words, add code and models, all of it matter.

The kernel does this five ways IN MEMORY today, as five registries (matter types, do ops, roles, reducers, and the verb past fold) that each re-implement the identity part and get populated out of band at module load. But everything else in the substrate is folded live from facts, never held in a registry, and a binding is no different. A registry is a cache, and the substrate does not trust caches as truth.

So there is no registry. The connecter is a FACT, folded live: `bindWord(name, descriptor)` lays a **declare-word fact** (the descriptor, its handlers carried as code matter refs), and the live word-set and their bindings ARE the fold of those facts. To know what a word does, you fold its word-facts, the same call that folds a being. disable is a new fact, on or off is the fold (the §3 instate-then-switch shape), and words stack because the fold composes a `can[]`.

roleWordRegistry already proves it in one corner: it binds role-words entirely through declare-word and disable-word facts with a per-branch fold, no Map. The work is to generalize that one fold to every word, and let the verb dispatch read the fold instead of the five Maps. The five Maps then become the seed bootstrap (the bottom turtle's bundled words) or fold away as I_AM's genesis declarations at boot. The only registry-ish thing that legitimately stays is the fold ENGINE and the base reducers, but those are host, the loom, not a registry of words. The in-world `do:declare-word` and the .word structural fold are the same act, laying a word-fact, not a sixth path.

## §3 Peering and the network are core, and switched at boot

Peering and all the network stuff is core. Its word goes in words/, its code goes in the factory; it moves inward out of the share, since networking is something every land has, not an opt in pack. Peering is the pipe; the share is what you move over it.

Core does not mean always on. Peering being declared means it is **instated**: a declared word is a fact forever (the words stack doctrine, 9.md). Whether it RUNS is separate:

- On boot the console operator (the boot console, not the retired portal view) chooses peering on or off, laying an on or off declaration over the instated word.
- If the boot console step is missed, it defaults to **on**.
- Turning it off later is not deletion, it is a new off fact; whether peering executes is the fold of those facts.

So instate is permanent, on or off is a new fact, run is a fold. The switch is a fact on the chain, not a config flag outside it. Any core word that can be turned off follows the same instate then switch shape.

## §6 Moment is not a noun

Being, space, matter, name are things in the world. A moment is the present itself, the now an act happens in and the vessel of the facts it leaves. It belongs with act and fact (the tense lane, `seed/past`), not with the nouns. A thin moment.word ("a moment is the present; an act happens in a moment; a fact is what the moment leaves") if it reads better, but it is tense, not a thing.
