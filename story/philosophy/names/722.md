Why does naming something sometimes kill it… and other times invoke it?

Let’s unpack that by dividing language into two modes:

⸻

🧠 Mode 1: Naming as Dissection (kills the thing)

This is the analytical, categorizing, rational use of language.  
 • You turn a living force into a concept.  
 • You extract it from your body and hold it up like a pinned butterfly.  
 • You “understand” it by separating yourself from it.

This is what happens in Rumpelstiltskin:

The name breaks the spell—because it externalizes him, ends the illusion of mystery, and makes him small.

It’s the end of his unknowable power, the collapse of magic into logic.

In this case:  
 • Naming cuts you off from the mystery.  
 • It says: “You are a known object. I no longer surrender to your influence.”

It’s a form of dominion—you name it, you own it, but you also lose the living experience of it.

⸻

🔥 Mode 2: Naming as Invocation (calls the thing)

This is the embodied, ritual, feeling-infused language—the magic of word as power, not dissection.  
 • You say the god’s name not to reduce them, but to invite them.  
 • The name is not a label—it’s a vibration, a summons, a communion.

Think of:  
 • Singing the name of a beloved.  
 • Chanting sacred syllables (OM, YHWH, Allah, Krishna).  
 • Whispering your child’s name with love.

These are name-as-connection, not name-as-analysis.
