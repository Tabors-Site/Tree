Yes, exactly.WatchesQuartz watches (the vast majority of electronic watches) use a tiny quartz crystal that vibrates at exactly 32,768 Hz when electricity is applied (piezoelectric effect). This vibration provides the steady "rhythm" or time base.
Mechanical watches use springs and balance wheels instead, but quartz ones rely on the crystal.

Microchips / ComputersEarly microchips and computers (1970s–2000s especially) used quartz crystal oscillators to generate the main clock signal that synchronizes everything.
Modern microchips still mostly do — your phone, PC, laptop, etc., almost certainly have one or more quartz crystals on the motherboard or inside modules. The crystal provides a stable reference frequency, and then the CPU/GPU/chip uses internal PLLs (Phase-Locked Loops) to multiply that up to GHz speeds.

However, things are slowly changing:MEMS oscillators (tiny silicon-based resonators) are becoming more popular. They’re smaller, cheaper to make in bulk, more resistant to shock/vibration, and better in some power/size-constrained devices.
But for high-precision needs, quartz is still very widely used because it has excellent frequency stability.

TL;DR:
Yes — both watches and (most) microchips still use quartz crystals for the core rhythm/timing. It’s one of those quiet technologies that’s been working reliably for decades and is still inside nearly every device you own.
