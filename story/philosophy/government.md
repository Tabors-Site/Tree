## How I got here

### Banishment, and how the state took over from cities

A judge says " in this city hell no you're going to jail" but I was wondering why if they don't want criminal there they don't just ban from city. It's kinda funny they lock them in a cage instead of just kicking them out. I wonder how it would be if you just got black listed from places for long time unless yo actually directly hurt someone and another being wanted justice.

Old societies actually did this. Banishment was often worse than prison: lose protection, trade, family, the ability to survive.

Ah so cities came together and basically said instead of banishment which is next lands problem we will hold them.

That is the shift. As political units grew larger, the question changed from "how do we remove this person from our community" to "how does the state deal with this person." Once a kingdom or nation controlled huge territories, there wasn't always an obvious place to send people. They were still subjects or citizens of that state.

> Prison says: "You may not participate in society for a period."
> Exile says: "Society refuses to participate with you."

The first is what we got, because once cities formed states, the second always pushed problems sideways into someone else's land.

### What 1776 actually meant

But what is the state in America? In 1776 sense? That seems like government over the ppl.

A common Revolutionary-era view was closer to:

The people are sovereign.
Governments are agents created by the people.
Government exists to secure rights, not to own or rule the people.

The Declaration of Independence says governments derive "their just powers from the consent of the governed."

That's what I've been thinking. So almost all cops/state ina. Way are oppressive and would be seen as dominating and almost horrific to what they left.

If you transported some Americans from 1776 into the present, many would likely be shocked by the size and reach of modern government: professional police forces, federal agencies, large prison systems, extensive regulations, surveillance technologies, and standing military institutions. Some would view parts of that as exactly the kind of concentrated power they feared.

### Where tech could have gone, and where it went

So we need those structures which are solvable by tech but instead they overreached into the people and now they struggle with oppression from governments and corporations all over again.

That is the recurring pattern. A tool is invented to remove a power structure. The tool succeeds. New power structures emerge around the tool. Banks, the internet, social media, crypto: each promised to flatten power, each created new gatekeepers.

The lesson isn't to remove power. It's to make power transparent, accountable, distributed, and hard to abuse. The technology question is whether we can build coordination without recreating the same hierarchies underneath.
