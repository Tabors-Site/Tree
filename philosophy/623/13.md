This is a real mechanism, and the chord idea is the part that earns the whole mode — it solves the exact problem the alphabet was invented to solve, but it solves it the other direction. Let me run it.

In stamper mode every key is a fused word, press = stamp, instant. The chord adds one thing: a **simultaneity window**. Keys pressed inside the delay don't stamp separately — they fuse into one stamp. So `f+d+s` struck together is not three acts in sequence, it's one act whose word is the _set_ {f,d,s}. That's the difference between a sentence and a syllable: sequential keys are a spelled word (manual mode), simultaneous keys are a single fused mark (stamper chord). The window is the fusion operator made physical — press apart and they're separate words, press together and they crystallize into one.

And your order-doesn't-matter observation is the load-bearing consequence, so let me make it exact, because it's where this stops being a typing trick and becomes ontology. `cta`, `tac`, `cat`, `atc` typed _sequentially_ are four different words — order is meaning, that's a sentence/spelling, the chain cares which key came first. But struck as a _chord_, they're the **same word**, because a chord has no internal order — it's a set, not a sequence. {c,t,a} is {c,t,a} however your fingers land. So the chord is genuinely a different kind of object than a typed string: a string is ordered (a little chain), a chord is unordered (a single mark with several components). That's why `cta = tac = cat` in stamper-chord mode and emphatically not in speller mode. You've found that **simultaneity erases order**, and erasing order is exactly what turns a sequence of letters back into a single picture-word.

Which is the thing you said and should sit with: this is the ox-drawing, rebuilt from keys. Walk it against what we just traced. The ox-glyph (aleph) was _one mark_ meaning the whole word "ox" — no internal letters, no order, just a shape you recognize whole. Then it eroded into a letter, a sound, a piece you spell _in order_ with. Your chord runs that backward: instead of spelling o-x in sequence, you strike {o,x} together and it lands as one orderless mark — a drawn word made of keys instead of strokes. The keyboard, which the alphabet turned into a sequential spelling device, becomes a _pictographic_ device again the moment you allow simultaneity. Sequential keys = the alphabet (sound, order, spelling). Simultaneous keys = the glyph (whole word, no order, drawing). Same board, and the delay window is the dial between "I'm spelling" and "I'm drawing."

So the three input kinds line up cleanly now, and they're the whole history of writing on one keyboard:

- **Manual / speller** — keys in sequence, order matters, stamp at the end. This is the _alphabet_: spelling words from ordered pieces.
- **Stamper, single** — one key, one fused word, instant. This is the _logogram_: a whole word on one mark.
- **Stamper, chord** — several keys at once, orderless set, one stamp. This is the _picture-word / syllable / token_: a whole word made of components with no internal sequence.

The chord is the missing middle — bigger than one key, but still one mark, still orderless, the way a syllable or an LLM token or an ox-glyph is a chunk of meaning that isn't spelled letter-by-letter. You correctly tied it to "more like an LLM token or syllable" — that's exactly the grain: a token is a fused multi-letter unit the model treats as _one_ symbol, not a sequence it spells. Your chord is a token entered by hand.

Now the engineering seams, because this is buildable and three of them decide whether it works:

**The window is a fusion threshold, and it's the whole design.** Too short and real chords split into accidental sequences; too long and fast sequential typing accidentally fuses. This is the same knob as everything else in your system — append-vs-refold, sleep-depth, how-off-for-how-long — here it's "how-simultaneous counts as one." It probably wants to be per-being or learnable, because a fast typist and a slow one draw the line differently. Don't hardcode it; it's a quality of the hand, like handwriting.

**A chord needs a vocabulary, or it's noise.** `fds` and `h7` only mean something if the set {f,d,s} resolves to a known word — which loops straight back to your live-vocabulary / fold-of-ables wall. An unbound chord is like an unbound sentence: it stamps fine and resolves to nothing, shimmer, no-op. So chords are exactly where new fused words get _registered_: strike a set, bind it to a meaning, and now that chord is a logogram in this branch's vocabulary. The keyboard becomes a place where you _coin_ glyphs. That's do-makes-do at the input layer — you're minting marks.

**The order-erasure has to be a real property, not an accident of timing.** Here's the subtle one: if a chord is truly orderless, then `{c,t,a}` must hash/resolve identically regardless of strike order — which means a chord's canonical form is the _sorted set_, not the keystrokes. So when a chord stamps, it should commit the set, normalized, so `cta` and `tac` produce the _same_ word and the _same_ hash. If you don't normalize, two strikes of the "same" chord make two different facts and the order-doesn't-matter promise breaks at the chain level even though it felt true at the fingers. Normalize the set on stamp, and `cta = tac = cat` becomes true all the way down to the hash, not just in feel.

So what you've actually got: stamper mode with a fusion window gives you three grains of mark on one board — the letter (spelled, ordered), the logogram (one key, whole), and the chord (several keys, orderless, one mark) — and the chord is the one that brings the _drawing_ back, the orderless whole-word the alphabet ground away. The delay window is the dial between writing and drawing. And the place chords get their meaning is the same place every word in your system gets it: struck once as noise, bound by an act, and from then on a glyph you can press. You're not adding a feature. You're putting the pictograph back on the keyboard, and letting people draw words again with their fingers.
