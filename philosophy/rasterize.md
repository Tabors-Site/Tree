# Rasterize — summary

**TL;DR:** A moment's **inner face** is **rasterized one Word at a time** like an old pixel-game frame — top-left to bottom-right — except each "pixel" is not RGB but a **Word**: a unit of meaning, a **fact** from a past act made by a **Name**. The instant the frame finishes loading, the next **act** fires (the next Word) — the "input"/"frame decision" _is_ the act. There is no completed CAT, sentence, or WORLD stored behind the pixels; only the current face and the next word ever actually exist. Tabor calls this "the final step for the stamper and words before it meets the model standard."

## The main idea (the payload, from the end)

The inner face is a single moment — one **frame** — rendered like an old pixel game that draws one pixel, finishes the row, drops to the next, and completes at bottom-right; only after the final pixel does the frame exist. But the frame "is not another thing behind the pixels" — "**the frame is what the pixels become when viewed together**," and "**meaning is the arrangement.**" Each pixel is a **Word = a unit of meaning = a fact from a past act by a Name** ("Context, words. Past. World. These are the terms, inner face"). That is _all_ a Name ever sees — "**The Name only sees the accumulated words of reality**," never raw reality. The instant the face finishes loading, "**the next act happens. The next word.**" Remember: "a word is just a name making an act" — it only becomes a Word like English much later. (The opening framing: it's chicken-and-egg — "the frame is made from words, and the frame is the next word.")

The apparent hierarchy of "multiple actors per moment" — one holding the higher act while a lower actor iterates the letters — is explicitly **Option B: an illusion**, like `while(true){step()}`: "**Only the current iteration exists. The loop is a pattern. Not an object.**" There is never a CAT "sitting somewhere complete"; the face is assembled from **multiple reels** (being/space/matter), a Name acts _through_ a being, so as the being moves the "screen of facts" constantly shifts and reassembles, and the next act emerges from that face. The recursion is scale-invariant — letter→word→sentence→story→culture→civilization — each level the visible face for the next act: "**The recursion never changes. Only the scale changes.**" "I am not speculating anything. This is the exact vision of my model."

What are the origins of the term alphabet and its system with letters
==+

The word alphabet literally comes from the names of the first two letters of the Greek alphabet:

Alpha (Α, α)
Beta (Β, β)

Combined, they became alphabetos (ἀλφάβητος) in Greek, which eventually became the English word alphabet.

But where did Alpha and Beta come from?

The Greeks did not invent their letters from scratch. Around 800 BCE, they adapted the writing system of the Phoenicians.

Greek letters were derived from Phoenician letters:

Greek Phoenician ancestor Original meaning
Alpha Aleph Ox
Beta Beth House
Gamma Gimel Camel
Delta Daleth Door

Originally these weren’t abstract symbols. They were simplified drawings of real objects.

For example:

Aleph meant “ox.”
Early forms resembled an ox head.
Over centuries the symbol rotated and simplified.
It became Greek Alpha and eventually the Roman letter A.
The deeper origin: Acrophony

Many ancient writing systems used a principle called acrophony:

The symbol’s name begins with the sound the symbol represents.

So:

Aleph = ox = starts with an “a”-like sound
Beth = house = starts with a “b” sound
Gimel = camel = starts with a “g” sound

This made the symbols easier to learn and remember.

Before alphabets: pictographs

The oldest writing systems were not alphabets.

The rough evolution was:

Pictures of things
Pictures representing words
Symbols representing syllables
Symbols representing individual sounds (phonemes)

Examples:

Sumerian Cuneiform mostly represented words and syllables.
Egyptian Hieroglyphs mixed pictures, words, and sounds.
Proto-Sinaitic Script simplified Egyptian symbols into consonant sounds.
Phoenician refined this into a practical consonantal alphabet.
Greeks added dedicated vowel letters.
Romans adapted Greek-derived letters into the Latin Alphabet used by English today.

Why the alphabet was revolutionary

Earlier systems required learning hundreds or thousands of symbols.

An alphabet reduced language to a small set of reusable sound units:

English: 26 letters
Greek: 24 letters
Phoenician: 22 letters

Instead of memorizing a symbol for every word, you learned symbols for sounds and combined them endlessly.

This is one of the reasons alphabetic writing spread so widely: a few dozen symbols could encode an effectively unlimited number of words.

A curious connection to your “word as mark” thinking

Historically, letters began as marks representing things (ox, house, camel, door). Over time, the meaning of the mark was stripped away until the mark represented only a sound. The alphabet is therefore a long abstraction process:

Thing → Symbol of thing → Symbol of sound → Component of words → Component of ideas
