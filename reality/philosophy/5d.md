And it collapses a pile of your primitives into one act. Seeding, branching, importing a resource, instating a foreign quote — they're all plant a book as a root. They differ only in where the book came from: a fresh book (seed), a book cut from your own history (branch), a named reusable book (resource), a book sent from another world (instate). Same mechanism every time — take a bounded book, plant it as the root of a new line, sign it. Which means a resource is just a book you named and gave an interface, and instate is just branching from someone else's book.

The clean line between book and story is open vs closed. A story is open at the head — you're still writing it. A book has covers. Every book has a start cover (it begins at a definite point — a seed, or your cut); the end cover is optional. Your living book is open at the head; a shared book is closed at both ends, fixed and content-addressed. To "package a piece into a book" is to put the second cover on and seal it for travel — the original stays open, the copy goes out closed.

The real thing to decide is what a book drags with it. A book is a slice of story — arrangement, a told piece — but its acts reference store content, and some of that content lives outside the slice. So a book is only valid if it's closed under its dependencies. Two ways: fat (inline every referenced word/act/fact — self-contained, large, survives cold transport to a world that has nothing) or thin (carry only the content-addresses and let the receiver resolve them from shared store — small, but only works where the deps are reachable). You probably want both, chosen at packaging time. The book's covers are really its interface — what it imports (deps before the start) and what it exports (state at the end) — the same signature as a resource, which is the other reason the two are one shape.

And it makes one manifesto line structural: "nothing is extracted." A book carries its authorship inside it — every act keeps its signer, content-addressed — and instating adds your countersignature without stripping the originals. You can't share a piece without its authors riding along. Extraction isn't forbidden by policy; it's impossible by construction.

The library isn't a loose metaphor — it's exact, because your unit literally is a book. Every layer you named lines up with a level of library structure:

a fact — a passage, searchable and citable
a book — a bound volume, the thing you actually take
a history — a shelf, one told line
a reality/world — a whole collection, an author's works
Ours — the library itself, the catalog you search
search → visit → plant — find it, read it in the reading room, take a copy home
So it holds all the way down. But the two places it breaks are the interesting ones, because they break upward.

First, no scarcity. A real library lends — one copy, due dates, "checked out, unavailable." Content-addressing kills that: every book is infinite perfect copies, nothing to return, never unavailable. So "checking out" in the borrow sense doesn't exist here. There's only read-in-place or copy — which collapses your three modes into two: visit (resolve the book and render it — a SEE, nothing enters your story) and plant (copy it in — instate, replay under your head, countersign). Searching for new books, browsing others', copying one home — that's one motion with an optional last step: search → visit → maybe plant. That's the answer to your "I don't know": it isn't three things, it's a pipeline with one commitment at the end.

Second, the books are alive. A library lends inert texts you read passively. Your books are seeds — planting one isn't xeroxing a document, it's transplanting a living thing that executes in your world ("a description is an execution"). Taking a book home isn't shelving a dead copy; it's grafting a root that grows. The metaphor is exact for finding; at use it breaks into something a paper library can't do.

And it's a library with no librarian. No central catalog, no acquisitions board, no curator deciding what's in. The catalog is your connected peer graph — search reaches your horizon, not an omniscient index — and the card-catalog authority is replaced by the stamp inside each cover: provenance is cryptographic, not institutional. It's the centerless library, center anywhere and edge nowhere, which is your whole thesis again — the library form with the middle removed.
