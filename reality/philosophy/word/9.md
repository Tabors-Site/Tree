# 9.md . word.word: the self-grounding root

> In the beginning was the Word.

The bootstrap problem, stated plainly. `verbs.word` declares "a verb is a word," but the word *word* is never grounded. The foundation descends from nothing. `verbTense.js` even names itself "the runtime projection of verbs.word," yet nothing folds verbs.word into it at boot (no `declarePast` caller), so the projection is a hardcoded mirror with no source. The language points at a root that was never written. This file writes it, and lays the plan to make the whole foundation descend from it, so the language becomes true of itself and drift becomes structurally impossible.

Lanes: the doctrine is Tabor + claude (2026-06-19); the build is engine/claude; the surface stays plain Word (Tabor's rule). This is a PLAN, not a landing.

## §0 The regress, and the bottom turtle

A self describing system cannot define everything in terms of other things. The definitions regress forever unless there is a base case that grounds itself. Turtles all the way down need a bottom turtle. `word.word` is that turtle, named.

We already solved this once, for the actor. "I am that I am" (Exodus) is the self rooting Name: the identity that refers to nothing but itself, the genesis that closes the regress so every other Name descends from it (the I_AM doctrine: one being, two natures, the genesis terms all I_AM's, never overwritten). `word.word` is the same move for the language: "In the beginning was the Word" (John), the self rooting Word, the definition that grounds itself, the genesis from which verb, act, fact, being, space, matter all descend.

The two are deliberately one shape, because John says so: "the Word was God." The self naming (I_AM, Exodus) and the self defining (word.word, John) are one primal act seen from two sides: who acts, and what can be said. Exodus bootstraps the actor; John bootstraps the language; together they are the genesis, and they ground each other. We are not inventing a new trick. We are applying the I_AM trick to the vocabulary.

## §1 The root line

`word.word` opens with the line that refers only to itself:

```
A word is a word.
```

That is the bottom. Everything else is "a X is a word," descending from this one self grounding root. The whole foundation, described in Word.

## §2 The rigor: self description is not self implementation

This is the load bearing distinction, the reason the plan is buildable and not a regress in disguise.

`word.word` can DECLARE the foundation. It cannot IMPLEMENT it. "A word is a word" describes; it does not stamp, parse, conjugate, or fold. The BEHAVIOR of the foundation primitives lives in the host (the kernel), where it always has.

This is our own axiom/theorem split (the resources are words doctrine: a composite word is a THEOREM that reduces to existing words; a code word is an AXIOM that bottoms out in host code behind the name, the trust boundary), applied to the floor:

- The FOUNDATION words are AXIOMS: declared in Word for self description, implemented in the kernel for behavior. A word, a verb, an act, a fact, a being, a space, a matter. Opaque past the word boundary; the host is what they DO.
- Everything ABOVE is THEOREMS: composite Word that reduces entirely to the foundation. Transparent, unfoldable, safe by construction.

So `word.word` is the Word level NAME for the host turtle standing on itself. You can make the system describe its own foundation. You cannot make the foundation out of nothing but Word. The host is the bottom turtle; `word.word` is what the turtle is called when it stands on itself.

What this buys: the foundation becomes self consistent and inspectable in Word, without pretending the kernel does not exist. The declaration and the implementation are two faces of one axiom, the way I_AM is both a Name (declared) and the reality key (implemented).

## §3 The descent

From the one root, the foundation:

```
A word is a word.

A verb is a word.
A verb has a present, the shape it takes in an act.
A verb has a past, the shape it takes in a fact.

An act is a word.    (the present: a doing)
A fact is a word.    (the past of an act: a done thing)

A being is a word.
A space is a word.
A matter is a word.
```

Each line is "a X is a word," each foundation word an axiom the kernel implements. `verbs.word` stops going off nothing: its "a verb is a word" finally hangs from "a word is a word." Everything the surface speaks now traces, line by line, back to the first line.

## §4 Tense at the root: act and fact

"Each word needs a past" bottoms out perfectly clean, because the two deepest words ARE their own tense, at the etymological floor:

- `act` = *agere*, the doing. The present.
- `fact` = *factum*, the done. The past.

`act` to `fact` is present to past, baked into the two words at the root. It was never arbitrary that a present act becomes a past fact: the words say so. So the rule "a verb forms its past from its present" has, at the very bottom, a pair of words that are already their own conjugation. The foundation's tense is not declared on top of it; it is the foundation.

This also seals the clause name reconciliation we have been living (by, through, of, act on the fact): `act` carries the present, `fact` the past, in their roots, and the chain is a sequence of *facta*, done things, the past of acts. The rename was the etymology surfacing.

## §5 The boot order: word.word first, self describe to the surface

Today the boot starts at genesis (I_AM, the spaces, the host). The runtime projections the surface needs (pastOf for tense, the verb set, the act/fact shapes) are hardcoded mirrors. `verbTense.js` DECLARED_PAST is the clearest case: it claims to be the projection of `verbs.word`, but nothing folds verbs.word into it.

The plan inverts the order. The boot reads `word.word` FIRST, before genesis uses anything it declares, and folds the foundation into the runtime:

1. `word.word` (and any foundation `.word` it grounds) is read and folded at the very start of boot, before, or as the first act of, genesis.
2. The fold POPULATES the runtime projections from the declarations: pastOf reads the declared pasts (`declarePast` called from the fold, closing the gap); the verb set, the act/fact shapes, the foundation kinds all come from `word.word`, not a hardcoded list.
3. Genesis then runs ON the self described foundation. I_AM, the spaces, the beings: all named in a vocabulary that was grounded first.
4. The surface (see, do, call, be, name, recall; the book; the roles) descends as composites on the foundation.

The hardcoded maps do not vanish overnight; they become the BOOTSTRAP that `word.word` describes, the axiom's implementation. But the SOURCE OF TRUTH moves to `word.word`. After the fold, `pastOf("drop")` is "dropped" because word.word says so, not because a JS map was edited. The map, if it stays, is the kernel's copy of what word.word declares, and the two are checked to agree (drift detection becomes a boot assertion).

## §6 Why drift becomes impossible

Drift is when the surface and the foundation disagree: when a word means one thing in the kernel and another in the Word. Today that is possible. I added "drop" to a JS map this session, and `verbs.word` knew nothing of it; the two could diverge silently. That is the exact failure this file ends.

After `word.word`:

- Every word has a declared past, descending from one root. Nothing goes off nothing.
- The runtime projections are FED by `word.word`, not hardcoded. The kernel's tense, verbs, and foundation kinds are the fold of the declarations.
- The boot can ASSERT that the kernel's axioms match `word.word`'s declarations. A foundation a verb declares but the host does not implement, or a host primitive `word.word` does not name, is a boot error.

So the language describes itself, end to end, from "a word is a word" to the live surface, and the only freedom left is to declare new words IN Word, which by construction trace back to the root. Drift is not policed; it is structurally absent.

## §7 The build plan

Phase 1 . Write the root.
- `word.word`: the genesis line (`A word is a word.`) plus the foundation descent (verb, act, fact, being, space, matter; each "a X is a word"; each with its past where it has one). Reuse the `verbs.word` grammar, which is already "a verb is a word; its past is Y."
- Decide one file or many (see §9). Recommend: `word.word` IS the root and the foundation, one file, the bottom; everything else is `.word`s above it.

Phase 2 . Wire the boot fold.
- Read `word.word` at the start of boot (`begin.js` / `genesis.js`), before the surface uses tense or verbs.
- Fold it: call `declarePast` for each declared past (closing the `verbTense.js` gap), register each declared verb, record the foundation kinds. The "runtime projection of verbs.word" comment becomes true.
- This is the boot wiring the registry to fold machinery flagged as next (verify-word-fold 8/8 already green); this roots it at `word.word` and wires it into boot.

Phase 3 . Move the source of truth.
- The hardcoded DECLARED_PAST (and any sibling hardcoded list of verbs or fact shapes) becomes derived from `word.word`, or kept only as the kernel's copy with a boot assertion that it equals the fold of `word.word`.
- Regular verbs (connect, open, serve, close) need no declaration (the -ed rule handles them); only irregular and consonant doubling pasts are declared in `word.word` (drop, make, see, give, take, and the rest already in verbs.word).

Phase 4 . The axiom anchor.
- Document, in `word.word` or beside it, which foundation words are AXIOMS (host implemented: word, verb, act, fact, being, space, matter, and see/do/call/be/name/recall) and which surface words are THEOREMS (composite). The line between them is the trust boundary.
- The host primitives stay in the kernel; `word.word` names them; the boot asserts they agree.

Phase 5 . Verify the descent.
- A test that boots from `word.word` and confirms the surface self describes: tense comes from the fold, the verbs come from the fold, the foundation kinds come from the fold, and the boot assertion (kernel equals word.word) passes. The book rendering a verb past it never hardcoded is the visible proof.
